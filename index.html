He Posted Himself as Jesus. Then He Attacked the Pope for Preaching Peace.

We write this with grief, not glee. What happened this past Sunday is not a political story to be scored. It is a spiritual wound — one that the Body of Christ did not ask for and cannot ignore.

The now-deleted Truth Social post: Trump depicted in white robes, laying hands on a sick man, bathed in golden light. He later claimed he thought it showed him as a doctor. (Image: Christian Century / RNS)

On Sunday, April 13, 2026 — Orthodox Easter Sunday — President Donald Trump posted an AI-generated image to Truth Social depicting himself as Jesus Christ: white-robed, hands outstretched in a healing pose, surrounded by Americana and divine light. The image was deleted by Monday morning after a firestorm of condemnation that united Catholics, Lutherans, Southern Baptists, charismatics, and evangelicals — including some of Trump's most ardent Christian supporters.France 24

When pressed by reporters, Trump claimed he "thought the image was of him as a doctor." Whether that is true, only he knows. What is true is that the image was created, approved, and posted — on the day Christians around the world celebrate the Resurrection of Jesus Christ. And many of the people who love this president, who prayed for him, who campaigned for him, found they could not look away from what that means.France 24

But the image did not emerge in a vacuum. It came hours after Trump launched a furious attack on Pope Leo XIV — the first American-born pope in the history of the Church — calling him "weak on crime" and a tool of the "radical left." The Pope's offense? Quoting the Prince of Peace. Leo had condemned Trump's threat to annihilate an entire civilization as "truly unacceptable," and called on the world's leaders, as Jesus did, to choose peace over the sword.Euronews

"Blessed are the peacemakers, for they will be called children of God."

— Matthew 5:9, The Beatitudes

For Bible-believing Christians, the sequence of events is deeply alarming: a world leader cloaks himself in the image of the Messiah, then attacks a Christian leader for proclaiming the Gospel message of peace. Scripture calls this idolatry — the exaltation of a human image in the place where only Christ belongs. And it calls those who use the language of faith as a tool of power false prophets: men who come in sheep's clothing. Jesus said we would know them by their fruit.

The reaction from the Christian community — across every tradition — was swift and clear. Rev. Tony Suarez, Trump's own longtime evangelical adviser, said the image "needs to be taken down immediately" and that it is "an offense to allude or depict that a human can compare to our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ."Roys Report Pastor Doug Wilson — Pete Hegseth's personal pastor and founder of the Reformed church network Secretary Hegseth attends — called it "blasphemous" and said Trump should "take it down and apologize."Roys Report Marjorie Taylor Greene, once Trump's most vocal evangelical ally in Congress, called it "more than blasphemy — an Antichrist spirit."Newsweek

Conservative Catholic activist Riley Gaines invoked Galatians directly: "God shall not be mocked."Salon Even Republican Senator Thom Tillis called the post "absurd."Irish Star

"I have no fear, neither of the Trump administration, nor speaking out loudly about the message of the Gospel."

— Pope Leo XIV, en route to Algeria, April 14, 2026

Pope Leo XIV responded with the calm authority of a man whose kingdom is not of this world. He pledged to keep preaching against war, for peace, for the dignity of every human being, "because the Church's witness is grounded in the peace of Christ, not in partisan interests."NPR

Three American Cardinals — Tobin of Newark, McElroy of Washington, and Cupich of Chicago — stood with him, appearing together publicly to reaffirm the Pope's moral authority. Cardinal McElroy went further, stating that Trump's war with Iran fails to meet the Church's ancient standard of a just war — a teaching rooted not in politics, but in Scripture and centuries of Christian ethics.TIME

Meanwhile, Vice President JD Vance — who publicly converted to Catholicism in 2019 — told the Vatican to "stick to matters of morality" and let the President handle war. Many pastors and theologians noted the painful irony: a Catholic VP telling the Pope that war is none of the Church's business, while the President posts himself as Jesus.TIME

"Do not be deceived: God cannot be mocked. A man reaps what he sows."

— Galatians 6:7

The Body of Christ is being asked a question. Not a political question — a spiritual one. When a leader wraps himself in the image of our Lord while wielding the sword, what does faithfulness require of those who follow the real Jesus? The Prophets had an answer. So did the Apostles. So did every generation of the Church that faced this test before us.

"The problem is not that Trump can't tell the difference between himself and Jesus. It's that too many of us can't."

— Russell Moore, Editor-in-Chief, Christianity Today

In the days that followed

The week did not narrow. It widened. On April 15, the President posted a second image of Christ — this one embracing him. On April 16, at the Pentagon, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth called the press "Pharisees" and opened an official briefing with a monologue adapted from the film Pulp Fiction, substituting himself for the character who recites a fabricated Bible verse before killing a man. On April 17, asked by CNN anchor Kaitlan Collins why he was fighting with the Pope, the President said the Pope had told him Iran could have a nuclear weapon. Collins corrected him on air: the Pope has said no such thing. That same day, the White House announced the President would read scripture from the Oval Office the following Tuesday. On April 18, Secretary Hegseth called the press "Pharisees" a second time — this time to dismiss reports that sailors deployed in the Iran war were running short of food.CNN Daily Beast

On April 21, the President sat down at the Resolute Desk, opened a Bible, and read 2 Chronicles 7:11–22 into a camera for two and a half minutes. The passage is beloved of the Christian right for one line: "If my people, which are called by my name, shall humble themselves, and pray, and seek my face, and turn from their wicked ways; then will I hear from heaven, and will forgive their sin, and will heal their land." The passage that follows — which the President also read — is a warning from God to kings who turn away: "I will uproot them out of my land... and this house, which is high, shall be an astonishment to every one that passeth by it." Neither he nor the organizers remarked on the second half. Scripture, when read aloud, is not a prop. It reads the reader.CBN News Video

And still, in the days that followed, the voices grew. At the University of Notre Dame — the most prominent Catholic university in the country, which gave Trump 59% of its local Catholic vote in 2024 — students, including self-described conservatives, called the image "textbook blasphemy." Notre Dame's own College Republicans issued a statement supporting the Pope and calling the war in Iran "not in American interests."CNN Russell Moore, the former Southern Baptist ethics chief and one of the most credentialed evangelical voices in America, wrote in Christianity Today that the image was "blasphemy" and that "the problem is not that Trump can't tell the difference between himself and Jesus. It's that too many of us can't."Christianity Today David French, writing in the New York Times, warned that some evangelicals, influenced by Trump, "won't unite with their Catholic brothers and sisters in response to Trump's blasphemy... but will instead turn against them."MSNBC

And the Pope? On April 18, aboard the papal plane from Cameroon to Angola, Leo was asked about the feud and declined to engage. "It was looked at as if I was trying to debate the president, which is not in my interest at all," he said. The next day, he prayed the Rosary at the Sanctuary of Mama Muxima — a Portuguese-built church that was a hub of the Atlantic slave trade — and said: "It is love that must triumph, not war."NBC News AP

"He hath shewed thee, O man, what is good; and what doth the Lord require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?"

— Micah 6:8

The question the Body of Christ is being asked has not gone away with the week. It has sharpened. The voices on this page — Trump's own evangelical adviser, the pastor of his Defense Secretary, a candidate for SBC president, a Lutheran bishop, three Cardinals, the editors of Christianity Today, the Notre Dame College Republicans, and the Pope himself refusing the role of antagonist — answer it in one direction: the image of Christ belongs to no man, and the Gospel of peace cannot be repealed.

Tucker Carlson to the New York Times: "What He's Saying Is, 'I'm More Powerful Than the Pope'"

In a 1,800-word New York Times piece published this morning, White House correspondent Katie Rogers reports that the President's recent rhetoric on faith has "threatened to splinter a powerful coalition of religious voters who returned him to the White House" — including white and Latino Catholics and Latino evangelicals, all of whom swung Trump's way in 2024. Three voices stand out.

Tucker Carlson, who tried to persuade Trump against bombing Iran and called his Easter Sunday "Praise be to Allah" post a mockery of faith, gave the Times the sharpest analytical frame yet of the entire arc: "We are watching Donald Trump intentionally transgress in ways that display power. What he's saying is, 'I'm more powerful than the pope.'" Carlson called the result a "caricature of Christianity" that "is melting away" key parts of the 2024 coalition. Trump has since called Carlson "low I.Q."

Rev. Dr. Gabriel Salguero, president of the National Latino Evangelical Coalition (several thousand churches), reported that church attendance is down as much as 30% in some congregations because of immigration enforcement raids. Latino evangelicals, he said, are "the quintessential swing voters," and many favor enforcement but not indiscriminate raids that have swept up pastors and congregants alike. "I feel very strongly that there will be a reckoning come midterms."

Robert P. Jones, founder of the Public Religion Research Institute, told the Times Trump is now "underwater with both white and nonwhite Catholics." A February Pew poll found Trump at 23% approval among Hispanic Catholic voters — who represent about 45% of American Catholics — and 52% among white Catholics. By contrast, 84% of Catholics view Pope Leo favorably. Jones flagged Wisconsin, Pennsylvania, and Texas as states where small Catholic shifts could decide midterm races.

A revealing detail from the Times' reporting on the Oval Office Bible reading: Bunni Pounds, founder of Christians Engaged and organizer of the "America Reads the Bible" event, told the Times she had no other reading in mind for Trump but the one he read — 2 Chronicles 7:11–22. The same passage was "shouted from a bullhorn during an attack on the U.S. Capitol on Jan. 6, 2021." Asked about the President's behavior, Pounds said: "We don't completely know where he is in his heart. But we do believe that he has at least a tenderness toward Scripture." New York Times

Louisiana: A Bishop Names the President in a Pastoral Letter — and Trump Country Catholics Are Listening

Louisiana is more than a quarter Catholic — among the highest concentrations in the nation. Trump won the state in three consecutive presidential elections, and a 2020 AP-NORC poll found 71% of Louisiana's registered Catholic voters voted for him. This morning, New Orleans Public Radio reported that Bishop Michael Duca of Baton Rouge sent a pastoral letter to his diocese — titled "Blessed Are The Peacemakers" — that named the President directly. Duca wrote that Trump's language is "troubling because it risks reducing the Holy Father to a partisan figure and further deepening divisions in an already fractured world." Speaking to WWNO, Duca did not soften: "My words to the people were certainly, 'We do not approve and have greater offense at the way in which our Holy Father has been portrayed as a politician, as someone who doesn't know what he's talking about. He knows exactly what he's talking about, but he's not a politician. He is the Vicar of Christ.'"

Retired U.S. Army Lt. Gen. Russel Honoré — who grew up not far from Pope Leo's family roots in New Orleans's Seventh Ward, and who spent 37 years in the Army — also went on the record. Asked about parishioners welcoming Trump's harsh words during the war: "There are people inside our community [saying] 'Yeah, yeah, yeah. I'm glad the president said that.' You mean you wanna see the president destroy a civilization? Do you really wanna see that?"

A Trump-aligned state representative, Michael Echols of north Louisiana — a Catholic convert running for federal office — offered the administration's working frame: that the Pope's message of peace and Trump's "peace through strength" can coexist. But even Echols admitted the friction is "uncomfortable for someone whose faith and politics are so important to him."

As Bishop Duca put it: "Peace. Blessed are the peacemakers." What Bucks County, Pennsylvania showed last week, Louisiana shows this week: the Pope's witness is reaching deep into Trump country, and bishops who would otherwise have stayed quiet are putting Trump's name in their pastoral letters. "As a Pastor, I Cannot Be in Favor of War" — Pope Leo's First Press Conference Since the Feud

On the flight from Malabo to Rome, closing an 11-day, four-nation, 18,000-kilometer tour of Africa that AP called "one of the newsiest papal trips in the history of popes on the road," Pope Leo XIV held a 20-minute press conference — his first extended media engagement since President Trump's attacks began in earnest. He spoke in English, Spanish, and Italian, answering questions on war, immigration, the death penalty, and the Church. The Washington Post's framing: Leo has "shed his previous image" and now speaks with "an assertive voice."

On the war in Iran: "As a pastor, I cannot be in favor of war. I would like to encourage all to make efforts to look for answers that come from a culture of peace and not from a place of hate and division." He said the U.S.-Israeli bombing has created "a chaotic situation for the global economy" and that "a whole population in Iran, of innocent people, is suffering because of this war." Asked about regime change, Leo refused the frame: "If there is regime change or no regime change, the question is how to promote the values in which we believe without the death of so many innocent people."

On the human cost: Leo revealed he carries a photograph in his pocket — of a Lebanese Muslim boy who had held a welcoming sign for the Pope at the end of 2025 and was killed in the war. He also cited a letter from the families of schoolchildren killed on the first day of the Iran war.

On the Iranian regime's killing of protesters (his first remarks on the topic): "I condemn all actions that are unjust. I condemn the taking of people's lives. I condemn capital punishment. I believe that human life is to be respected."

On immigration: Countries have "the right to establish rules at their borders," he said — but "when people arrive, they are human beings, and they deserve the respect that every human being deserves. We must treat human beings in a humane way and not treat them often times worse than pets at home or animals."

On the Church's moral priorities: "We tend to think that when the church is talking about morality that the only issue of morality is sexual. And in reality, I believe there are greater and more important issues — such as justice, equality, freedom of men and women, freedom of religion — that would all take priority before that particular issue." CNN NCR Washington Post AP
