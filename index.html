Lead Story

The President Who Posted Himself as Jesus, Then Attacked the Pope

In the span of a single weekend, Donald Trump shared an AI-generated image depicting himself as Christ — then launched a furious broadside against the actual head of the Catholic Church for daring to call for peace.

The now-deleted Truth Social post depicted Trump in white robes with a golden backdrop. The White House later claimed it was meant to show him as a doctor.

On Sunday, April 13, 2026, President Donald Trump posted an AI-generated image to Truth Social that appeared to depict him as Jesus Christ — shown in white robes, laying hands on a man in a hospital bed, surrounded by Americana and golden light. The post was deleted by Monday morning. When asked about it, Trump claimed he thought it showed him as a doctor.

Within hours of the image going viral, Trump pivoted to a lengthy attack on Pope Leo XIV — the first American-born pope in Church history. Trump called the pontiff "weak on crime" and accused him of being a vehicle for the "radical left."

The trigger? Pope Leo had publicly denounced Trump's threat to destroy Iran's "whole civilization" as "truly unacceptable," and has consistently used his pulpit to call for peace as the Iran war has escalated. Leo was en route to Africa on a four-nation peace mission when the attacks landed.

"I have no fear, neither of the Trump administration, nor speaking out loudly about the message of the Gospel."

— Pope Leo XIV, aboard a flight to Algiers, April 14, 2026

The political fallout was swift — and cut across ideological lines in ways few predicted. Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni, one of Trump's closest European allies, broke ranks to call his criticism of the Pope "unacceptable."

Perhaps more damaging, the loudest condemnations came from within Trump's own former base. Marjorie Taylor Greene, once among his most loyal supporters, called it "more than blasphemy — an Antichrist spirit." Conservative commentator Tucker Carlson described using Easter as a backdrop for war rhetoric as "unchristian." Candace Owens said Trump was "very clearly under demonic influence."

Vice President JD Vance — a Catholic convert — attempted damage control, urging the Vatican to "stick to matters of morality" and leave U.S. policy to the President. Critics noted the breathtaking irony of a VP telling the Pope to stay in his lane while the President posted himself as the Messiah.

American Catholic leaders piled on. Cardinal Tobin of Newark accused Trump of a "troubling lack of respect for the faith of millions." Cardinal McElroy declared the Iran war does not meet Catholic standards for a "just war." Republican Senator Thom Tillis called the Jesus post "absurd." As of Monday, April 14, Trump has refused to apologize.

The AI image depicted Trump in white robes laying hands on a sick man, bathed in golden light and American imagery. After deleting it, Trump claimed he "thought it was a doctor image." Sen. Tillis called the post "absurd." Rep. Davidson called it "very troubling."
